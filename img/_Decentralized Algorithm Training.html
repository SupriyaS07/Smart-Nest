Answer
Decentralized algorithm training is gaining traction as a standard method for addressing large-scale decision-making problems and training machine learning models, especially with the growing need for privacy and efficiency1. This approach involves spatially distributed computing units that collaboratively train machine learning models without sharing local private data, using wireless communication networks and cloud-based computing platforms1. However, the fusion of blockchain and AI implementation into existing systems poses multiple barriers that need to be addressed before realizing its maximum potential2. :
  • Decentralized learning algorithms are being employed in various applications, including training predictive keyboards, healthcare diagnostic models, and intelligent transportation systems1.
  • Decentralization is a promising method of scaling up parallel machine learning systems3.
  • Federated learning (FL) has become a popular paradigm for training distributed, large-scale, and privacy-preserving machine learning systems4.
  • Decentralized algorithms can significantly reduce training time by leveraging multiple parallel computing nodes1. For example, DeepMind developed a massively distributed architecture for training deep Q-networks (DQNs) that achieved comparable performance but ten times faster than single-GPU DQN implementations for most Atari games1.
:
  • Both blockchain and AI require substantial processing resources, and the combination can exacerbate liability issues, especially in large-scale deployments2. Blockchain networks often suffer from limited transaction throughput, while AI as a Service demands huge computing power for training and inference2.
  • Integrating blockchain and AI poses challenges due to their structural differences; AI relies on centralized computation, whereas decentralized technology distributes data across nodes2.
  • Balancing the immutable and transparent nature of blockchain with the need for AI to access large, potentially sensitive datasets is challenging2. Maintaining data privacy while ensuring operational transparency is a key concern2.
  • The merger of blockchain and AI can overload existing infrastructure required for massive data processing and storage2.
  • FL can be vulnerable to data confidentiality, integrity, and availability threats4.
  • FL requires multiple rounds of interactions where updated model parameters are iteratively exchanged, which can slow down the distributed training of complex models4.
  • Data generated by edge devices in FL can be highly non-IID (independent and identically distributed) and exhibit significant statistical differences4.
:
  • Embracing new consensus tactics like proof of stake and adopting layer two solutions can increase scalability without compromising decentralization2.
  • Implementing inventive design tactics can effectively solve architectural differences between blockchain and AI2.
  • Using solutions like zero-knowledge proofs and federated learning can allow data validation without revealing the data, safeguarding privacy while ensuring operations2.
  • Experimenting with change activities to boost the pressure on block networks and creating more effective consensus processes can take advantage of available resources2. Knowledge-based decentralized training algorithms can optimize data transfer and minimize costs, especially when scaling with many nodes5.
  • DFL solutions have emerged to mitigate the single-point-of-failure risks and man-in-the-middle attacks associated with centralized orchestration in classical FL4. In DFL, all FL clients cooperate and communicate without a central server4.
